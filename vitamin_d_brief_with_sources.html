Goal: justify physician-directed biweekly IM vitamin D for selected deficient athletes when oral therapy has not produced durable repletion.

Why this matters in basketball

Athlete context
  • Vitamin D deficiency is highly prevalent in athletes, with the review citing about 56% prevalence overall.
  • Jumping-sport athletes such as basketball players were reported with 51.3% vitamin D inadequacy in pooled data.
  • Indoor athletes, busy schedules, seasonal changes, and high training demands are recognized risk factors.
Clinical relevance
  • Low vitamin D status is associated with impaired bone health, muscle weakness, fatigue, and stress-fracture risk.
  • The athlete review notes target 25(OH)D above 40 ng/mL for fracture prevention, with no clear sports benefit above 50 ng/mL.
  • The strongest rationale is injury-risk reduction and biochemical repletion, not guaranteed direct performance improvement.
Key message: Persistent values in the 13–27 range sit squarely inside deficiency or insufficiency bands used in athlete literature, making structured repletion medically relevant.

What the evidence supports

↑ 25(OH)D
Randomized and observational studies show IM cholecalciferol raises serum 25(OH)D and can sustain repletion over months.
Use case
Best suited when oral therapy, adherence, absorption, or scheduling barriers prevent durable correction.
Not proven
The athlete literature does not establish injectable vitamin D as a universal performance intervention.
Randomized comparison

An open-label RCT comparing 300,000 IU oral versus IM cholecalciferol found both routes effective and safe; oral rose faster at 3 months, while results were similar by 6 months, supporting route selection based on compliance and practicality.

Durability signal

A high-dose IM study reported normalization of serum 25(OH)D in all participants with levels remaining above 50 nmol/L through 12 months, while also flagging the need to watch urine calcium and mild hypercalcemia.

Proposed biweekly IM protocol language

Eligibility
  • Documented 25(OH)D below team target despite appropriate oral supplementation.
  • Indoor or high-load basketball athletes with repeat low values and limited opportunity for consistent oral adherence.
  • No known contraindication such as hypercalcemia, granulomatous disease risk, or other clinician-identified exclusion.
Protocol framing
  • Use IM cholecalciferol as a short-term physician-directed repletion pathway for selected athletes.
  • Biweekly administration should be individualized to baseline level, prior response, body size, and product-specific prescribing guidance.
  • Do not frame the protocol as universal weekly dosing or as a guaranteed ergogenic aid.
Sample governing-body wording

“For athletes with persistent vitamin D deficiency or insufficiency despite appropriate oral supplementation, the medical staff may consider a physician-supervised biweekly intramuscular cholecalciferol repletion protocol, provided baseline evaluation and interval laboratory monitoring are performed and treatment is held for calcium-related adverse findings.”

Monitoring and safety language

Baseline
  • 25(OH)D
  • Serum calcium
  • Creatinine / renal function
  • Phosphorus
  • PTH when clinically indicated
Follow-up
  • Repeat calcium and 25(OH)D after each repletion cycle.
  • Check sooner if polyuria, nausea, constipation, confusion, weakness, or dehydration develops.
  • Consider urine calcium in athletes at stone-risk or with aggressive replacement needs.
Hold criteria
  • Hypercalcemia
  • Hypercalciuria
  • Unexpected renal dysfunction
  • Suspected vitamin D toxicity
The published IM literature supports efficacy with generally acceptable short-term safety, but also reports mild hypercalcemia and increased urine calcium in some participants, which is why the proposal should emphasize supervision, stop criteria, and interval reassessment.
